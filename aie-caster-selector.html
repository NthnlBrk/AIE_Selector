02

How heavy is the load?

Enter the total loaded weight (equipment + cargo) and the number of casters you'll use. We'll calculate capacity per caster with a standard safety factor.

One caster may briefly carry more than its share. On uneven floors, in turns, or going over thresholds, weight transfers — sometimes onto three wheels instead of four. The industry rule is total load ÷ (caster count − 1), or for heavier duty, total ÷ caster count × 1.33. We use 1.33 by default. If you're rolling daily on rough floors, call us — a higher factor may be warranted.

Where will it roll?

Floor and environment drive wheel material more than any other variable. Be honest about the worst conditions, not the average ones.

The floor wins. A soft polyurethane wheel on rough concrete will flat-spot in months; a hard phenolic on tile will mark and chip. Match wheel hardness inversely to floor hardness for protection, or directly for capacity. We'll bias the recommendation accordingly.

How does the caster attach?

Mount type is dictated by your equipment, not preference. If you're not sure, choose "help me figure it out" — we'll guide you on the call.

Top plates bolt to a flat surface and carry the most load — default for carts and equipment. Threaded and grip-ring stems screw or press into a tube or socket — common on chairs, dollies, and tubular frames. Expanding adapters wedge into the inside of a tube. Bolt-hole uses a single through-bolt. Mount type doesn't affect capacity directly, but mismatched mounts are the #1 reason replacements don't fit.
05

How should it move?

Caster configuration controls tracking and turning. Brakes control parking and runaway prevention.

2 swivel + 2 rigid tracks straight and steers from the swivel end — best for tow lines, push carts, and stable handling. 4 swivel turns in place — best for tight aisles, but harder to push straight. Diamond pattern (1 swivel + 4 rigid + 1 swivel on six-wheel rigs) pivots around the center rigid for long carts.
